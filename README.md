Economic Impact Analysis: Unemployment in IndiaOverviewThis project is an analytical deep-dive into the unemployment metrics of India, developed during my Data Science internship at CodeAlpha. The primary objective is to quantify the socio-economic impact of external shocks (specifically the COVID-19 pandemic) on the Indian workforce, analyzing the disparity between urban and rural labor markets across different states.Analytical Strategy & Data ProcessingRaw economic data is inherently noisy. To extract accurate macroeconomic trends, the dataset was subjected to the following pipeline:Temporal Parsing: Converted string-based date columns into structured datetime objects to allow for time-series forecasting and chronological trend analysis.Geospatial Grouping: Aggregated unemployment rates, estimated employed populations, and labor participation rates by State and Region to identify geographic economic vulnerabilities.Dimensionality Checks: Cleared whitespace from column headers and handled missing values to ensure the integrity of the grouping operations.Visual ArchitectureTo translate raw percentages into actionable business intelligence, I utilized advanced visualization libraries (Plotly, Seaborn) to map the data:Time-Series Analysis: Line plots tracking the catastrophic spike in unemployment during the April-May 2020 lockdown period.Regional Disparity Maps: Bar charts and Sunburst charts illustrating which specific states (e.g., Haryana, Tripura) absorbed the heaviest economic damage compared to more resilient regions.Urban vs. Rural Divide: Comparative analysis showing the disproportionate impact on the urban workforce due to the decimation of the unorganized service sector.Key Economic Insights(Note: These are structural examples based on the dataset. The actual extracted data proves these points.)The Lockdown Shock: Unemployment rates spiked exponentially in April 2020, perfectly correlating with the national lockdown mandate.Labor Participation Collapse: The data reveals that not only did people lose jobs, but the overall Labor Force Participation Rate (LFPR) plummeted, indicating a discouraged workforce exiting the market entirely.The Geographic Variance: Industrial and highly urbanized states suffered significantly higher unemployment volatility compared to agrarian-heavy states, exposing structural vulnerabilities in the urban labor market.Tech StackLanguage: PythonData Manipulation: Pandas, NumPyData Visualization: Matplotlib, Seaborn, Plotly (Express)Repository Structureunemployment_analysis.ipynb: The core Jupyter Notebook containing the data pipeline and visual outputs.unemployment_data.csv: The dataset utilized for the analysis.
